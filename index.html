The Effect of Lactobacillus reuteri on the Leaf Length of Rapid-Cycling Brassica rapa

Background

Agriculture covers nearly 37% of Earth’s land, and while fertilizers are vital for food production, they also pollute soil, water, and the atmosphere. Farmers often misuse fertilizers, leading to inefficiency, economic hardship, and reduced yields. In response, scientists are investigating environmentally friendly alternatives such as probiotics, including Lactobacillus reuteri (L. reuteri). Probiotics can improve microbial communities, nutrient cycling, and plant disease resistance. Previous studies demonstrate the benefits of lactic acid bacteria in forming symbiotic relationships that promote plant health. This study investigates whether L. reuteri could serve as a sustainable alternative to fertilizer by measuring leaf length, width, and count in Brassica rapa.

Rationale

The rationale for this experiment lies in the urgent need to reduce reliance on chemical fertilizers, which damage ecosystems and contribute to climate change. Since many farmers struggle with applying fertilizers correctly, identifying accessible, natural alternatives is critical. L. reuteri, a probiotic with known health benefits in animals and humans, may improve plant growth by enhancing soil microbiomes. Testing different concentrations of this bacterium allows us to explore its potential to promote leaf development compared with fertilizer. If successful, L. reuteri could offer a safer, eco-friendly, and cost-effective solution to support global food security while reducing environmental harm.

The Goal

Probiotics like L. reuteri have long been studied for their role in human and animal health, but their influence on plants remains less understood. The plant microbiome plays a vital role in nutrient exchange, stress tolerance, and disease prevention, making beneficial microbes a promising tool for sustainable farming. Investigating how L. reuteri affects plant morphology provides valuable insight into whether this bacterium could extend its known benefits beyond humans. Since most existing research centers on compost or other plant growth–promoting bacteria, this study addresses a critical knowledge gap by evaluating how probiotics may function directly within agricultural systems.
